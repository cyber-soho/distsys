Monitoring vs. Observability

Welcome to Week 12. This section explores the fundamental shift in mindset required for modern distributed systems. You will learn the critical distinction between simply watching known metrics and building a system that allows you to ask novel questions when unpredictable failures occur.

🔍

Monitoring

"Is the thing I expected to break, broken?"

  • ✓ Watching a known set of indicators (CPU, RAM, disk).
  • ✓ Pre-built dashboards.
  • ✓ Tells you THAT something is wrong.
🕵

Observability

"Why is checkout slow only for users in Spain on Tuesdays?"

  • ✓ Asking new questions from the outside.
  • ✓ No need to ship new code to investigate.
  • ✓ Helps you find out WHY something is wrong.

Why the distinction matters

1

Distributed systems fail in ways nobody predicted at design time.

2

You cannot pre-build a dashboard for every failure mode.

3

You need the raw signal (logs/metrics/traces) and the tools to slice it at query time.

The Three Pillars

This section breaks down the three fundamental data types that make observability possible. You will interact with each pillar to understand its specific role, its strengths, weaknesses, and most importantly, how the "Correlation ID" binds them all together to form a complete narrative of a system request.

📜 Logs

Discrete time-stamped events ("X happened at T")

Best At:

Forensics, audit trails, answering "what exactly did the user click?"

Worst At:

Aggregation, fast counting at immense scale.

📈 Metrics

Numeric measurements over time (counters, gauges, histograms)

Best At:

Trend graphs, alerting, answering "is p99 latency rising?"

Worst At:

High-cardinality detail (e.g., trying to track per-user or per-request data points).

🔏 Traces

Causal record of a single request across many services

Best At:

Answering "*Where* exactly in the call graph is the latency occurring?"

Worst At:

Long-term storage. Due to massive volume, traces are usually sampled (not all are kept).

🔗

The Glue: Correlation IDs

Every incoming request gets a trace_id (a UUID). That single ID propagates through the entire system.

↔
Attached to Metrics (labels)

Primary Key of Traces

Result: From any one signal, you can pivot to the other two.

Prometheus Architecture

Governed by the CNCF, Prometheus utilizes a Time-Series Database (TSDB) and the PromQL query language. Its most significant architectural decision is that it pulls data; it does not get pushed to.

Why Pull Beats Push:
  • Health by definition: If scrape fails, target is down.
  • Service discovery friendly.
  • No client-side back-pressure needed.

Syslog and journald

Linux relies on two main logging systems that often run side-by-side. journald collects structured, binary logs from systemd units, which can then be forwarded to syslog for long-term text archival.

# Most useful command you will learn this week
$ journalctl -u nginx.service
# Tail-follow recent errors
$ journalctl -f --since "10m ago" -p err

Alertmanager

An alert is a promise that a human will be woken up. Alertmanager's job is to protect humans through grouping, inhibition, silencing, and routing.

The 3 Properties of a Valid Alert
  • 1 Actionable: There is something a human can do about it.
  • 2 Symptom-based: Describes user-visible pain, not internal cause.
  • 3 Documented: Every alert links to a runbook.

Rules & Best Practices

Observability is the layer you debug *with*; it must never be the layer you debug. Review these hard-won rules from production engineers regarding alerting and the essential glossary terms for exams and professional environments.

⚠ 5 Rules of Alerting

1. Alert on symptoms, not causes. "Checkout error rate > 1%" beats "Redis CPU > 80%".
2. Every alert links to a runbook. No runbook → no alert.
3. Not actionable at 3 AM = Not a page. Send it to a dashboard or a digest instead.
4. Track your alert volume. A page-per-week per engineer is healthy. A page-per-day is a bug.
5. Post-mortems update alerts. Every incident either tightens, loosens, or deletes a rule.

The Golden Signals

  • USE (For Hosts): Utilisation, Saturation, Errors.
